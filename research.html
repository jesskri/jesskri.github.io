My in vitro meat research has three main aims: 1) Close the gap in similarity between in vitro meat and livestock meat, 2) Develop muscle cell isolation methods for different species, such as pigs and lobsters, 3) Develop a suitable bioreactor system for in vitro meat production.




How do you improve the similarity of in vitro meat to livestock meat?

Muscle (meat) is grown from the process of myogenesis: where individual muscle stem cells fuse together to form large elongated muscle fibers. In vitro meat products must undergo myogenesis the way muscle cells do in the body.

My research focuses on enhancing in vitro meat myogenesis through using 2D cell cultures and 3D tissue cultures. By studying myogenesis, we can grow bigger and better muscle with the taste and texture of livestock meat.


Why pig and lobster in vitro meat products?

Pigs are one of the smartest animals on the planet (along with humans, elephants, and dolphins), but also one of the most delicious (I mean, who doesn't love bacon?). Therefore making an in vitro meat option for pork and bacon is imperative.


On the other hand, lobster meat has a very high price-per-pound, making it an easier target to provide a competitive in vitro meat price in comparison to beef.


How do you grow in vitro meat outside of an animal?
Growing cells and tissues outside of the body requires an ex vivo approach using bioreactors. Currently, no bioreactor system accommodates all the physiological needs for growing in vitro meat. The ExVivo BioRxr bioreactor is designed to overcome these design limitations. The ExVivo BioRxr improves nutrient and oxygen delivery to in vitro meat by acting as an artificial heart that pumps artificial blood into the muscle. This system will "exercise" the muscle to improve its development, the way muscle is exercised in the body to increase its strength. This bioreactor system will grow bigger and better in vitro meat than current industry methods.
